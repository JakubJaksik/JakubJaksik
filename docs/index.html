Implementations & hardware

built from spec · C++ & Verilog

Hardware · the one I'm proudest of

AES-XTS-256

A full AES-256 in XTS mode in synthesizable Verilog for the Intel Cyclone V. The whole datapath is there: the key schedule, encrypt and decrypt rounds, the XTS tweak layer with α-multiplication in GF(2128), a control FSM sequencing the block pipeline, and self-checking testbenches. XTS is the mode disk encryption uses, so correctness on the last, partial block matters — that's handled too. It met timing on the Cyclone V with room to spare.

02Blockchain

Where the cryptography meets the wire

Trading pulled me onto Solana and EVM, and the questions I care about there are the same ones I care about in hardware: what actually happens, measured, at the lowest level. No strategies here — just the plumbing, instrumented.

Measurement study · live write-up

solana-sender-analysis

How does a transaction actually get into a Solana block? I raced seventeen transaction senders head to head with synthetic self-transfers, timed everything in Proof-of-History ticks rather than noisy wall-clock, and ran the statistics properly. The result surprised me: the fastest sender is not the one that wins. Inclusion is a routing and peering problem, decided per data-center and per leader — not on the wire. Rust tools for the measurement, Python for the analysis, and a full illustrated write-up.

Why ticks, not milliseconds

method

Clocks drift and “on chain” is fuzzy when you only see your own copy of the stream. Proof-of-History is a hash chain the leader ticks forward at a near-constant rate, so the chain itself becomes a ruler every observer agrees on. Measuring inclusion in ticks and hashes gives a deterministic distance with no clock sync — the honest number.
