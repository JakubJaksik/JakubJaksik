01Blockchain

Solana and EVM, at the low level

This is the day job, and it is also the part I would do for fun anyway: trading systems on Solana and EVM, where a few milliseconds and a routing decision are the difference between landing a transaction and missing the block. I care about that plumbing, and I would rather measure it than argue about it.

Measurement study · its own write-up

solana-sender-analysis

How does a transaction actually get into a Solana block? I raced seventeen transaction senders head to head with synthetic self-transfers, timed everything in Proof-of-History ticks rather than noisy wall-clock, and ran the statistics properly. The result surprised me: the fastest sender is not the one that wins. Inclusion is a routing and peering problem, decided per data-center and per leader, not on the wire. The whole thing has its own illustrated write-up.

Scored, not eyeballed

statistics

The race is judged properly: Wilson confidence intervals on every win-rate, McNemar for paired win and loss, a Bradley‑Terry model for sender strength, and a logit for what predicts inclusion. Adding “which sender reached this leader” lifts the fit from an R² of 0.01 to 0.40, while stake barely correlates at all.

Why ticks, not milliseconds

method

Clocks drift and “on chain” is fuzzy when you only see your own copy of the stream. Proof-of-History is a hash chain the leader ticks forward at a near-constant rate, so the chain itself becomes a ruler every observer agrees on. Measuring inclusion in ticks and hashes gives a deterministic distance with no clock sync, the honest number.

Where I trained, and where I am heading

Cryptography is my background and my long game. When a scheme catches my attention I implement it to really understand it, and a few of those ended up on FPGA back in my studies. Lately the interest is moving toward post-quantum and zero-knowledge, on ordinary hardware this time. Repos for the new work are on the way; below is what exists so far.

Current research

where my reading and writing go now

Post-quantum lattices

research

Working through the lattice problems the next generation of public-key crypto rests on: SIS and LWE, the reductions that make them trustworthy, and the schemes built on top once RSA and elliptic curves no longer hold up against a quantum machine.

current focus repo soon

Zero-knowledge proofs

research

Proving a statement is true while revealing nothing else. The part I find most interesting is exactly where it meets the chains I work on day to day: succinct proofs that let a blockchain verify heavy computation it never had to run.

current focus repo soon

Implementations

studies & hobby · C++ and Verilog

AES-XTS-256

Verilog

A full AES-256 in XTS mode in synthesizable Verilog for the Intel Cyclone V: key schedule, encrypt and decrypt datapath, the XTS tweak layer with alpha-multiplication in GF(2128), a control FSM, and self-checking testbenches. It met timing on the Cyclone V with room to spare. The one from this list I am still proud of.

BLAKE3

C++

The BLAKE3 hash implemented straight from the specification: the chunk tree, parent-node compression and extendable output, not just the round function. Checked against the official test vectors.

PRESENT

C++

PRESENT, the lightweight block cipher (ISO/IEC 29192-2): an 80-bit key, 31 rounds, a 4-bit S-box and a pure bit permutation. A small, clean study in how much you can get from very little silicon.
